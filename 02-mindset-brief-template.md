## The 3-line brief template

Before you type anything into Claude, fill in these three lines. Copy this, fill in the blanks, and paste it at the top of every message.

```
I need: [what you want Claude to do]
This is for: [who will read or use it]
Good looks like: [what a great result means to you]
```

That's it. Three lines. Most people skip all three and wonder why Claude sounds generic.

---

## Step-by-step: watch the difference

This is exactly what the reel demonstrates. Try it yourself right now.

### Step 1 — Open Claude and type the bad prompt

```
Write me a proposal.
```

You'll get something that reads like a template from 2012. Generic headings. Filler sentences. Nothing that sounds like your business.

### Step 2 — Now type the 3-line brief instead

```
I need: a proposal for monthly corporate lunch catering
This is for: an HR manager at a tech company in Karachi
Good looks like: professional, one page, highlights hygiene standards and reliability, ends with a clear next step
```

Notice what happens. Claude stops guessing and starts writing *for your actual situation.*

### Step 3 — Add the push-back prompt (optional but powerful)

Before you even send your brief, add this one line at the end:

```
Before you start, ask me anything you need to know to do this well.
```

Claude will pause and ask you 2–3 targeted questions — things like:
- "What's the budget range you're working with?"
- "Do you want to include references or testimonials?"
- "Should this be sent as an email body or a PDF attachment?"

Answer those questions. Then Claude writes the final version.

**The output you get after this process is almost always usable on the first try.**

## Full worked example (catering proposal)

**What you send:**
```
I need: a proposal for monthly corporate lunch catering
This is for: an HR manager at a tech company in Karachi
Good looks like: professional, one page, highlights hygiene standards and reliability, ends with a clear next step

Before you start, ask me anything you need to know to do this well.
```

**What Claude asks back:**
```
A few quick questions before I draft this:

1. What's the rough budget per person per meal, or should I leave pricing flexible?
2. Do you have a specific cuisine focus (Pakistani, continental, mix)?
3. Is this for daily lunch, or specific days of the week?
```

**You reply:**
```
Budget around 800–1200 PKR per head. Mix of Pakistani and continental. Daily Monday to Friday.
```

**Claude then produces:** A full one-page proposal with your pricing range, cuisine options, a hygiene section, and a "next step" paragraph at the end — ready to send.

No editing required.

## The "push back" prompt (save this)

Use this whenever Claude jumps to an answer without asking questions first:

```
Before you start, ask me anything you need to know to do this well.
```

You can also use these variations:

```
What information do you need from me before writing this?
```

```
What are you assuming about my situation? Ask me if you're not sure.
```

```
Don't start yet. What would help you write a better version of this?
```

Each one does the same thing: it slows Claude down and turns it from a guesser into a collaborator.

## 3 common mistakes + fixes

**Mistake 1: Being too vague**
- Bad: `"Write me an email"`
- Fix: Tell Claude who it's for, what the goal is, what tone you want, and how long it should be

**Mistake 2: Asking for everything at once**
- Bad: `"Write me a full marketing strategy, social posts, and email campaign"`
- Fix: One task at a time. Finish the strategy first, then say "Now write 3 Instagram captions based on this."

**Mistake 3: Accepting the first draft**
- Bad: Copy-pasting the first thing Claude gives you
- Fix: Reply with one of these — Claude improves fast when you push back:
  - `"Make it shorter"`
  - `"Make it warmer, less corporate"`
  - `"Try a completely different angle"`
  - `"The second paragraph is weak — rewrite just that part"`

**Mistake 4: No context about the reader**
- Bad: `"Write a LinkedIn post about my new product"`
- Fix: Tell Claude who follows you. `"My LinkedIn audience is HR managers and startup founders in Pakistan. They respond to practical advice, not hype."`

## The 3-line brief — more examples

**Freelancer sending a follow-up to a cold client:**
```
I need: a follow-up message after sending a proposal 5 days ago with no response
This is for: a small business owner who may be busy or hesitant
Good looks like: friendly, not pushy, keeps the door open, under 80 words
```

**Teacher creating a quiz:**
```
I need: 5 multiple-choice questions on the water cycle
This is for: Grade 6 students, first time learning the topic
Good looks like: simple language, one clearly correct answer per question, no trick questions
```

**Job seeker writing a cover letter:**
```
I need: a cover letter for a marketing coordinator role
This is for: a hiring manager at a Pakistani e-commerce company
Good looks like: confident but not arrogant, shows I understand digital marketing, under 200 words
```

## Why this works (the mental model)

Think of Claude like a talented freelancer you just hired — someone smart, fast, and capable.

If you say: *"Do the thing."* — they'll guess what you mean and deliver something average.

If you say: *"Here's the situation, here's who it's for, here's what good looks like, and here are my questions before you start"* — they'll ask the right clarifying questions and deliver something you can actually use.

Claude isn't underperforming. Most people are just under-briefing it.
